## The Great Citadel

**Status:** Current canonical version  
**Versioning model:** Full-copy standalone Codex version  
**Canonical world name:** The Great Citadel  
**Source:** Supersedes Codex v1.1 — Entry Hall Edition  

---

# 1. Core Vision

The Great Citadel is a vast medieval world of knowledge, memory, media, discovery, and reflection. Within it, information is not browsed; it is physically explored through rooms, halls, gardens, doors, maps, alcoves, symbols, and realms.

The Codex is the living record of the Citadel. It defines the architecture, rooms, realms, visual language, rendering rules, and currently accepted canonical design decisions.

The Great Citadel should feel ancient, lived-in, warm, human-scaled, and coherent. It should feel like a place of knowledge that is inhabited and cared for, not a cold or oversized monument.

# 3. Versioning System and Saving Protocol

Every Codex version is a complete standalone copy of the full document.

A version should include:

- All foundational rooms
- All defined realms
- All symbols
- All rendering rules
- All accepted amendments
- All current architectural standards
- A version number
- A version title
- A changelog section

No version depends on another version to be understood.

Branches may experiment, but only full Codex copies are promoted into the MAIN archive.

## Local and GitHub Save Protocol

Local Codex files are working drafts and may be overwritten during editing.

The Codex version number does not change for local-only edits.

The no-overwrite-history rule applies only when saving or publishing the Codex to GitHub.

When the user asks to “save the Codex to GitHub,” the Codex version number must increment, a changelog entry must be added, and the previous state must be preserved through Git history.

# 4. Foundations and Room Connections

## Foundation Role

The Foundations are the stable entry sequence of The Great Citadel.

They provide arrival, welcome, orientation, preparation, and transition into the deeper architecture of knowledge.

The Foundations do not function as a grand maze. They are the clear beginning path into the Citadel.

## Retired Room Names

The following former room names are retired from the active Codex:

- Front Entry
- Hallway

Their functions are now absorbed into the unified canonical room:

- Entry Hall

## Canonical Foundation Sequence

Maps of the Foundations are north-oriented.

```text
NORTH

Great Hall
    │
Foundational Door
    │
Small Library
    │
Entry Hall

SOUTH
```

## Main Foundation Path

The main visitor path is:

```text
Entry Hall → Small Library → Foundational Door → Great Hall
```

This path represents:

```text
Arrival → Welcome → Orientation → Understanding → Exploration
```

## Entry Hall

The Entry Hall is the southernmost entrance and first threshold space of The Great Citadel.

It combines the former roles of Front Entry, Entry Hall, and Hallway into one canonical room.

The Entry Hall serves as:

- The arrival point into The Great Citadel
- The first welcoming space for a new user
- The threshold between the outside world and the ordered interior
- The primary foundational passage
- The orientation path toward the Small Library

The Entry Hall connects directly to the Small Library.

The Garden branches from the Entry Hall and does not interrupt the primary path.

## Garden

The Garden branches from one side of the Entry Hall.

It is a place of calm, meditation, reflection, and mental restoration.

The Garden is not part of the main north-south Foundation sequence.

It is an optional branch space connected to the Entry Hall.

Garden connection:

```text
Entry Hall → Garden
```

Features:

- Stone pathways
- Carefully placed trees
- Benches
- Lanterns
- Moss-covered stones
- Water feature
- View of distant mountains

The water feature may be a reflecting pool, fountain, stream, or small waterfall. The sound of moving water provides a calming presence.

One side of the Garden opens toward distant mountain ranges beyond the Citadel. No pathways lead directly to the mountains. They serve as a reminder that the world is larger than the Citadel itself.

The Garden represents:

- Balance
- Reflection
- Perspective
- Patience
- Mental clarity

## Small Library

The Small Library is the final preparatory room before entering the Great Hall.

It represents understanding before exploration.

It is a compact, warm, human-scaled study room that prepares the visitor for the Great Hall.

The Small Library is not a hub.

It is a simple linear transitional room between arrival and exploration.

## Foundational Door

The Foundational Door connects the Small Library to the Great Hall.

It occupies the southern spoke of the Palace Sigil within the Great Hall and is integrated into the southern wall of the Great Hall.

The Foundational Door is one of the eight primary openings of the Great Hall.

Unlike the other seven primary openings, it does not lead outward into a knowledge realm or an antechamber.

It leads back to the Foundations through the Small Library.

Connection:

```text
Small Library → Foundational Door → Great Hall
```

## Great Hall

The Great Hall is the central navigation chamber of The Great Citadel.

It is the symbolic and navigational heart of exploration.

All major journeys into the greater Citadel begin here.

The Great Hall connects southward back to the Foundations through the Foundational Door.

Foundation return path:

```text
Great Hall → Foundational Door → Small Library → Entry Hall
```

## Great Hall Realm Connections

The Great Hall contains eight primary openings.

Seven lead toward major realms of knowledge through antechambers.

One leads back to the Foundations.

| Direction | Opening | Connection |
|---|---|---|
| North | Science Door | Hall of Discovery → Science Realm |
| Northeast | Philosophy Door | Philosophy Antechamber → Philosophy Realm |
| East | Arts Door | Arts Antechamber → Arts Realm |
| Southeast | Personal Door | Personal Antechamber → Personal Realm |
| South | Foundational Door | Small Library → Entry Hall |
| Southwest | Inventor's Door | Inventor's Antechamber → Inventor's Realm |
| West | Media Door | Media Antechamber → Media Realm |
| Northwest | History Door | History Antechamber → History Realm |

## Antechamber Connection Principle

Major realm doors do not open directly into fully developed realms.

Each primary realm door from the Great Hall opens first into an antechamber.

An antechamber is a transitional room that introduces the identity, atmosphere, symbols, and major paths of its realm.

Antechambers serve as orientation spaces between the Great Hall and deeper realm destinations.

The Hall of Discovery is the first canonical antechamber.

It belongs to the Science Realm and is entered through the Science Door.

## Foundational Door Exception

The Foundational Door is the exception to the Antechamber Connection Principle.

It does not lead to an antechamber.

It leads back to the Foundations through the Small Library.

# 5. Entry Hall

The Entry Hall is the southernmost entrance and first interior threshold space of The Great Citadel.

It combines the former roles of Front Entry, Entry Hall, and Hallway into a single canonical room.

The separate room names “Front Entry” and “Hallway” are retired from the active Codex.

The unified canonical room name is:

- Entry Hall

## Core Identity

The Entry Hall represents:

- Arrival
- Welcome
- Orientation
- Invitation
- First understanding
- Transition into the architecture of knowledge

It is the first place a visitor experiences when entering The Great Citadel.

It should reassure the visitor that they are entering a meaningful, cared-for place of learning, memory, and discovery.

## Spatial Role

The Entry Hall connects directly to the Small Library.

It serves as the threshold between the outside world and the ordered interior of The Great Citadel.

It also serves as the primary foundational passage space.

The Entry Hall should not feel like a grand chamber. It should feel like a small welcoming passage that gently receives the visitor and guides them inward.

The Garden branches from the Entry Hall and does not interrupt the primary path.

## Core Spatial Rule

The Entry Hall should be longer than it is wide.

It should read more like a narrow welcoming hall or passage than a broad chamber.

Its proportions should emphasize forward movement into the Citadel.

## Scale Rule

The Entry Hall should be small in human scale.

It should feel intimate, sheltered, practical, and personal.

It should be only as wide as necessary for comfortable passage, rather than expansive or ceremonial.

The room should feel like a place a single visitor can immediately understand upon arrival.

## Passage Width Rule

The Entry Hall should be just wide enough for a person to walk through comfortably.

It should not feel grand, vast, or open like the Great Hall.

It should feel like a threshold corridor that gently receives the visitor and guides them inward.

## Furnishing Rule

The Entry Hall should contain small tables on each side.

These tables may hold welcoming or orientation objects such as:

- Lanterns
- Books
- Small plants
- Bowls
- Notes
- Symbolic objects
- Simple hospitality items

The tables should reinforce warmth, welcome, and human presence.

## Coat and Jacket Rule

The Entry Hall should include a place to hang a coat or jacket.

This may take the form of:

- Wall hooks
- A wooden rail
- A small rack
- A carved hanging bar

This feature should make the Entry Hall feel inhabited, practical, and welcoming to a new arrival.

## Visual Character

The Entry Hall should be narrow, warm, cozy, and human-scaled.

It should feel ancient, lived-in, and cared for.

Defining features may include:

- A long narrow forward path
- Small side tables
- A coat or jacket hanging place
- Warm lantern light
- Stone walls softened by wood
- Carved timber beams
- Small plants
- Vines or moss where appropriate
- A modest floor emblem marking arrival
- Wall inscriptions suggesting inquiry, humility, and orientation

The Entry Hall should be welcoming rather than overwhelming.

It should feel like the Citadel is receiving the visitor personally.

## Material and Mood Rule

The Entry Hall should continue the broader material language of The Great Citadel:

- Stone
- Timber
- Carved wood
- Brass
- Bronze
- Leather
- Parchment
- Warm light
- Plants
- Vines
- Moss

Wood should play a strong role in softening the stone architecture and making the space feel cozy.

The room should feel practical, old, warm, and inhabited.

## Symbolic Elements

A foundational inscription may appear in the Entry Hall:

> Enter not to possess knowledge, but to be shaped by it.

An alternate shorter motto may appear where space is limited:

> Arrival begins inquiry.

Other inscriptions may emphasize welcome, humility, curiosity, and careful attention.

## Welcome Rule

The Entry Hall should give the feeling of welcome to a new user.

The emotional tone should include:

- Welcome
- Safety
- Warmth
- Curiosity
- Invitation
- Orientation

The Entry Hall should suggest that the visitor has arrived somewhere meaningful, but not intimidating.

It should feel like the beginning of a personal journey into The Great Citadel.

## Rendering Rule

When rendered, the Entry Hall should appear as a narrow welcoming passage with a clear forward path.

The image should communicate:

- Long, narrow proportions
- Small human scale
- Cozy warmth
- Side tables
- A place to hang a coat or jacket
- A welcoming atmosphere for a new arrival
- A clear path forward into the Citadel

The viewer should stand within the Entry Hall, not outside it.

The rendering should avoid making the Entry Hall look like a broad ceremonial chamber.

It should feel like a human-scaled threshold corridor.

# 6. Small Library

The Small Library is the final preparatory room before entering the Great Hall.

It represents understanding before exploration.

It is a compact, warm, human-scaled study room that prepares the visitor for the Great Hall.

The Small Library is not a hub.

It is a simple linear transitional room between arrival and exploration.

## Core Role

The Small Library serves as:

- The final preparatory room before the Great Hall
- The place of orientation before exploration
- The room where the visitor encounters the Instruction Manual and Guest Book
- A room containing the Codex as a reference source
- The transition between the Entry Hall and the Foundational Door

Connection sequence:

```text
Entry Hall → South Door → Small Library → North Door → Foundational Door → Great Hall
```

## Contents

The Small Library contains:

- An Instruction Manual and Guest Book
- The Codex
- Introductory collections
- Maps
- Symbol references
- Historical records concerning The Great Citadel
- Reading tables
- Lecterns
- Shelves
- Reference books
- Writing tools
- Map tables
- Lamps or lanterns

## Two-Door Principle

The Small Library has exactly two canonical doors.

1. North Door
2. South Door

No additional primary doors are canonical for the Small Library.

The Small Library is not a branching room.

Its canonical layout is linear and north-south.

It should clearly communicate:

- South = Entry Hall
- North = Foundational Door / Great Hall

## South Door

The south door is located on the south wall.

It connects directly to the Entry Hall.

Connection:

```text
Entry Hall → South Door → Small Library
```

The south door is the arrival connection into the Small Library.

## North Door

The north door is located on the north wall.

It connects onward to the Foundational Door and then to the Great Hall.

Connection:

```text
Small Library → North Door → Foundational Door → Great Hall
```

The north door is the forward connection toward exploration.

## Small Library Door State Rule

Both canonical doors in the Small Library should normally be shut.

This applies to:

- The north door leading toward the Foundational Door and Great Hall
- The south door leading back toward the Entry Hall

The doors may imply connection and direction, but they should not normally stand open in standard renderings.

A shut door helps preserve the Small Library’s quiet, enclosed, preparatory atmosphere.

## Viewing Directions

The Small Library has exactly two canonical viewing directions for standard room renderings:

1. Facing North
2. Facing South

No other primary viewing directions are canonical for standard room renderings of this room.

## One-Door Visibility Rule

Only one primary door should be visible at a time in a standard rendering of the Small Library.

A canonical rendering should show either:

- The north door
- The south door

but not both at once.

A north-facing rendering should show the shut north door only.

A south-facing rendering should show the shut south door only.

This preserves the Small Library’s identity as a simple linear room and avoids turning it into a visible circulation diagram.

## Spatial Character

The Small Library should feel:

- Compact
- Warm
- Quiet
- Human-scaled
- Focused
- Preparatory
- Lived-in
- Welcoming but more studious than the Entry Hall

It should feel quieter and more focused than the Entry Hall.

Where the Entry Hall welcomes the visitor, the Small Library helps the visitor understand where they are before proceeding onward.

## Material and Atmosphere

The Small Library should continue the broader material language of The Great Citadel:

- Stone
- Timber
- Carved wood
- Brass
- Bronze
- Leather
- Parchment
- Warm light
- Books
- Maps
- Plants
- Vines
- Moss where appropriate

Wood should play a strong role in softening the room and making it feel cozy.

The room should feel ancient, lived-in, practical, and cared for.

## Instruction Manual and Guest Book

The large prominent display book in the Small Library is not the Codex.

The large display book is a combined:

- Instruction Manual
- Guest Book

It serves as the visitor’s practical introduction to The Great Citadel and as a record of arrival.

It may include:

- Welcoming guidance for new visitors
- Orientation notes
- How to use the Citadel
- Suggested starting points
- Simple rules of conduct
- Reflections or signatures from prior visitors

It should feel practical, welcoming, and modestly ceremonial.

It should help bridge the transition from arrival to exploration.

The large Instruction Manual and Guest Book should be placed in the northeast corner of the Small Library.

It must not block the north door.

It should feel important and welcoming, but it should not obstruct the room’s linear north-south movement.

## The Codex in the Small Library

The Codex remains one of the important contents of the Small Library, but it is not the large display book used to greet visitors.

The Codex may appear as:

- A protected reference volume
- A shelved master text
- A smaller lectern book
- A curated reference source within the room

The large visible book used to greet the visitor is the Instruction Manual and Guest Book.

## Rendering Rule

When rendered, the Small Library should clearly communicate:

- That it is a linear room
- That it has exactly two canonical doors
- That only one door is visible at a time
- That the visible door is shut
- That it is warm, compact, and human-scaled
- That the Instruction Manual and Guest Book is a defining feature
- That the Instruction Manual and Guest Book is placed in the northeast corner
- That the Instruction Manual and Guest Book does not block the north door
- That the Codex may be present, but is not the prominent large display book
- That the room prepares the visitor for the Great Hall

A north-facing rendering should emphasize preparation for exploration and show the shut north door.

A south-facing rendering should emphasize return toward arrival and orientation and show the shut south door.

# 7. The Great Hall

The Great Hall is the central navigation chamber of The Great Citadel.

It is the symbolic and navigational heart of exploration.

All major journeys into the greater Citadel begin here.

## Purpose

The Great Hall serves as:

- Realm selector
- Orientation hub
- Knowledge navigation space
- Symbolic center of the Citadel

## Architecture

The Great Hall is circular and organized around the Great Knowledge Compass.

It contains:

- Palace Sigil
- Great Knowledge Compass
- Eight primary openings
- Domain medallions
- Door tracks
- Ancient stonework
- Brass mechanisms
- Banners where appropriate

## The Eight Primary Openings

The Great Hall contains eight primary openings:

1. Media Door
2. Science Door
3. History Door
4. Philosophy Door
5. Arts Door
6. Personal Door
7. Inventor's Door
8. Foundational Door

Seven lead to major realms of knowledge through antechambers.

One leads to the Foundations.

## Eight-Spoke Alignment Principle

The geometry of the Great Hall is derived from the Palace Sigil.

The Palace Sigil is not merely a symbol. It is the architectural blueprint upon which the Great Hall is constructed.

Each primary opening aligns with one spoke of the Palace Sigil:

| Direction | Opening |
|---|---|
| North | Science Door |
| Northeast | Philosophy Door |
| East | Arts Door |
| Southeast | Personal Door |
| South | Foundational Door |
| Southwest | Inventor's Door |
| West | Media Door |
| Northwest | History Door |

The wheel defines the Chamber. The Great Hall is the Palace Sigil made architectural.

## Great Knowledge Compass

The Great Knowledge Compass is the floor of the Great Hall and one of the oldest known artifacts in the Citadel.

It functions as:

- Compass
- Map
- Knowledge engine
- Historical record

At its center is the Palace Sigil.

Its spokes extend outward through:

1. Palace Sigil
2. Domain Medallion
3. Door Track
4. Primary Opening

The visitor should be able to visually trace a route from the center of the Hall to any opening.

# 8. Palace Sigil

The Palace Sigil is the highest symbol of The Great Citadel.

It represents:

- Navigation
- Discovery
- Learning
- Perspective
- The journey through knowledge

## Form

The Palace Sigil takes the form of a simple eight-spoked ship's helm inspired by traditional Buddhist wheel symbolism.

Characteristics:

- Circular form
- Eight spokes
- Strong silhouette
- Balanced geometry
- Immediate recognizability

The design should feel:

- Ancient
- Timeless
- Functional
- Calm
- Purposeful

## Symbolic Meaning

The Palace Sigil represents guidance rather than destination.

The Citadel does not dictate what visitors should learn. It provides pathways through knowledge.

The wheel symbolizes:

- Direction
- Choice
- Exploration
- Progress
- Self-guided discovery

The Palace Sigil is not a symbol of knowledge. It is a symbol of navigating knowledge.

The Hall of Discovery is defined by the relationship between its floor and ceiling.

## Vertical Knowledge Principle

The floor represents accumulated understanding.

The ceiling represents mystery and exploration.

Visitors stand between them.

## Map of Understanding

The floor of the Hall of Discovery contains a large inlaid map known as the Map of Understanding.

Unlike the Great Knowledge Compass in the Great Hall, this map is not primarily a navigation device. It is a visual representation of scientific knowledge and its relationships.

Materials:

- Stone
- Brass
- Bronze
- Inlaid wood

#### North — Astronomy

Represented through:

- Planetary paths
- Star charts
- Celestial diagrams
- Orbital patterns

The map guides visitors toward the Astronomy Door.

#### West — Biology

Represented through:

- Branching trees
- Evolutionary pathways
- Ecological relationships
- Growth patterns

#### East — Logic

Represented through:

- Geometry

- Pattern networks
- Logical relationships

The center of the Map of Understanding contains the Compass Rose of Discovery.

It serves as:

- Orientation marker
- Symbol of exploration

Materials:

- Brass
- Bronze

- Inlaid metalwork

Codex principle:

The Celestial Vault represents the unknown.

It serves as:

# 12. The Eight Alcoves of Discovery

The Eight Alcoves of Discovery serve as introductions to the paths of scientific inquiry.

The alcoves are not destinations. They are previews.

Each alcove provides a glimpse of the knowledge found beyond the door it accompanies.

## Alcove Ownership Principle

Each primary door in the Hall of Discovery owns two alcoves.

The contents of those alcoves reflect the theme of the associated door.

Every door is visually surrounded by its own neighborhood of learning.

## Layout

```text
                NORTH

         Astronomy Alcove

          Astronomy Door

         Astronomy Alcove


Biology Alcove      Logic Alcove

 Biology Door        Logic Door

Biology Alcove      Logic Alcove


          Science Alcove

           Science Door

          Science Alcove

                SOUTH
```

## Astronomy Alcoves

The two alcoves associated with the Astronomy Door contain:

- Planet models
- Celestial globes
- Star charts
- Constellation diagrams
- Astrolabes
- Orbital maps
- Lunar cycle displays
- Navigation instruments

Purpose: to introduce the study of the cosmos.

Visual character: brass instruments, celestial spheres, and astronomical artifacts.

## Biology Alcoves

The two alcoves associated with the Biology Door contain:

- Living plants
- Ferns
- Mosses
- Vines
- Botanical specimens
- Herb collections
- Seed archives
- Small trees and growth displays

Purpose: to introduce the study of life.

- Geometric constructions
- Mathematical models
- Tessellations
- Pattern demonstrations
- Mechanical puzzles
- Brass calculating devices

## Science Alcoves

The two alcoves associated with the Science Door contain:

- Observation journals
- Research notes
- Measuring instruments
- Experimental sketches
- Scientific records
- Unanswered questions

Purpose: to introduce inquiry itself.

These are the first alcoves encountered by visitors entering from The Great Hall.

Visual character: active investigation and curiosity before specialization.

# 13. Alcove Architecture

## Alcove Integration Principle

Alcoves are architectural recesses built into perimeter walls.

They are not:

- Freestanding displays
- Kiosks
- Islands
- Detached structures
- Furniture placed within the center of a room

Alcoves should always appear as part of the room's perimeter architecture.

## Perimeter Arc Principle

Alcoves occupy the same architectural arc as the doors they accompany.

The room perimeter should read as a continuous sequence of:

```text
Door → Alcove → Wall → Alcove → Door
```

## Center Line Visibility Rule

When rendering a room from visitor perspective, only alcoves located on the visible hemisphere of the room should be shown.

Alcoves located behind the viewer should not be visible.

If a wall is behind the visitor, its alcoves are behind the visitor.

Only the visible arc of a room should be shown.

# 14. Universal Rendering Standards

These standards apply to all major rooms, halls, realms, antechambers, and destinations unless overridden by a room-specific rule.

## Visitor Perspective Principle

All rooms should be rendered from the perspective of a visitor physically present within the space.

The viewer should feel:

> I am standing in this room.

not:

> I am looking at a diagram of this room.

## Central Viewing Principle

When rendering a room from visitor perspective, the viewer should be positioned at or near the geometric center of the room unless a room-specific rule overrides this.

Only the viewing direction changes.

## Forward Hemisphere Rule

The viewer should only see the portion of the room in front of them.

Architecture behind the viewer remains unseen.

## Limited Door Rule

When practical:

- Minimum visible doors: 1
- Preferred visible doors: 2–4
- Maximum visible doors: 4

Visitors should never see the entirety of a room's navigation structure at once.

Room-specific standards may impose stricter limits.

## Partial Floor Rule

Major floor features should normally be only partially visible.

Examples:

- Great Knowledge Compass
- Map of Understanding
- Future realm maps
- Navigation mosaics

The viewer should feel they are standing upon the artifact rather than observing it from above.

## Visible Ceiling Rule

Whenever a room contains a significant ceiling feature, that feature should be included when practical.

## No Omniscient Perspective Rule

Avoid:

- Bird's-eye views
- Floating camera angles
- Ceiling-level viewpoints
- Impossible perspectives

Exceptions:

- Maps
- Blueprints
- Architectural diagrams
- Codex reference illustrations

## Discovery Rule

A visitor should not fully understand a room from a single image.

Every rendering should imply hidden details, unseen destinations, and future discoveries.

## Neighborhood Rule

When a door is visible, the surrounding architecture should communicate the nature of the destination beyond.

Examples:

- Biology Door → living plants and botanical collections
- Astronomy Door → planets, celestial globes, and star instruments
- Logic Door → geometry, mathematics, and pattern displays

## Continuity Rule — Revised

All rooms should feel as though they belong to the same civilization.

The Great Citadel should feel ancient, lived-in, warm, human-scaled, and coherent.

Rooms should avoid feeling overly vast, cold, empty, or monumental unless a specific room requires grandeur.

Common materials:

- Stone
- Timber
- Carved wood
- Brass
- Bronze
- Leather
- Parchment
- Water
- Living plants
- Vines
- Moss

Subtle Time Traveler modifications may appear throughout the Citadel.

## Cozy Human Scale Rule

Rooms should generally feel comfortable to occupy.

Even when the architecture is old, ceremonial, or important, most rooms should retain a smaller human scale.

Preferred qualities:

- Warmth
- Shelter
- Intimacy
- Readable proportions
- Places where a person could sit, study, rest, or reflect
- Wood details that soften the stone architecture
- Lanterns, hearth light, or warm indirect illumination where appropriate

The Citadel should feel like a place humans live, study, and return to, not only a monument to be observed.

## Wood Emphasis Rule

Rooms should make greater use of wood.

Wood may appear as:

- Beams
- Railings
- Doors
- Wall panels
- Shelves
- Tables
- Benches
- Ceiling ribs
- Carved screens
- Decorative trim
- Warm architectural framing

Stone remains foundational, but wood should add warmth, texture, and human presence.

## Plant and Vine Integration Rule

Plants, vines, moss, and living greenery may appear throughout the Citadel.

Some walls may include climbing vines, small plants, or signs of nature reclaiming the architecture.

Plant life should feel integrated, not random.

It may suggest:

- Age
- Care
- Memory
- Restoration
- Reflection
- The relationship between knowledge and living systems

Heavy plant growth should be strongest in gardens, biology-related spaces, older corridors, and rooms connected to reflection or restoration.

## Archaeology Principle

Every room should show evidence of age and history:

- Repairs
- Additions
- Modifications
- Patina
- Worn stone
- Updated mechanisms

Nothing should appear newly constructed.

## Room Identity Principle

Every major room should possess at least one defining architectural feature.

Examples:

| Room | Defining Feature |
|---|---|
| Great Hall | Great Knowledge Compass |
| Hall of Discovery | Map of Understanding and Celestial Vault |
| Small Library | Instruction Manual and Guest Book; Codex reference source |
| Garden | Water Feature and Mountain View |
| Entry Hall | Human-scaled welcome passage; side tables; coat/jacket hanging place |

## Codex Principle

The Citadel is experienced, not observed.

Every rendering should place the visitor inside the world rather than outside it.

# 15. Great Hall Rendering Standards

## Purpose

The Great Hall is not a map.

The Great Hall is an architectural experience.

A rendering should make the viewer feel physically present within the chamber rather than observing it from above or from outside.

## Visitor Position Rule

The viewer shall stand at or near the geometric center of the Great Hall.

The viewer is assumed to be a traveler standing upon the Great Knowledge Compass.

The viewer should never appear outside the room.

## Directional Rendering Rule

Every Great Hall rendering must specify a viewing direction.

Examples:

- Facing North
- Facing Northeast
- Facing East
- Facing Southeast
- Facing South
- Facing Southwest
- Facing West
- Facing Northwest

The rendering should depict only what a visitor would naturally see when facing that direction.

## Forward Hemisphere Rule

Only the forward half of the chamber should be visible.

The viewer should not see architecture behind themselves.

Doors located behind the viewer should not be shown.

## Limited Door Visibility Rule

The Great Hall contains eight primary openings, but visitors should never see all eight at once.

Visible doors:

- Minimum: 1
- Preferred: 2–3
- Maximum: 4

A rendering that reveals all eight doors is considered non-canonical.

## Great Knowledge Compass Rule

The Great Knowledge Compass is experienced from within.

The viewer stands upon it.

Only a portion of the compass should normally be visible.

The compass should disappear beneath the viewer and extend beyond the frame.

The viewer should never see the entire compass from a normal standing perspective.

## Eye Height Rule

Camera height should approximate human eye level.

Preferred height:

- 5.5 to 6.5 feet above floor level

Avoid:

- Bird's-eye views
- Ceiling perspectives
- Floating viewpoints
- Architectural cutaways

## Architectural Scale Rule

The Great Hall should feel larger than the viewer.

Emphasis should be placed on:

- Height
- Stonework
- Arches
- Brass mechanisms
- The Palace Sigil
- The Great Knowledge Compass

The hall should feel ancient, massive, and lived-in, while still remaining part of the broader warm and human-inhabited Citadel.

## Door Presentation Rule

Doors should appear as architectural destinations.

Door labels should not appear floating in space.

Written labels may exist as carved stone, brass plaques, inscriptions, banners, or architectural ornamentation.

The room itself should communicate identity through architecture rather than signage.

## Discovery Rule

A visitor should not understand the entire Great Hall from a single image.

Each rendering should imply:

- Hidden destinations
- Additional doors beyond view
- Greater scale than currently visible
- Future discoveries

The Great Hall should invite exploration rather than explain itself completely.

## Facing North Example

When standing near the center of the Great Hall facing North:

Visible elements may include:

- Science Door at center
- Portions of the History Door and Philosophy Door
- Upper architecture
- Part of the Great Knowledge Compass
- Palace Sigil details
- Banners related to Science, History, or Philosophy

Not normally visible:

- Foundational Door
- Media Door
- Arts Door
- Inventor's Door
- Personal Door

These remain behind the viewer or outside the forward viewing arc.

## Canonical Principle

The Great Hall is experienced as a traveler.

It is never rendered as a complete diagram.

# 16. Great Hall Banner Standards

## Purpose

Banners in the Great Hall serve as architectural identity markers, not modern signs.

They help communicate the nature of nearby doors, realms, and directional paths while preserving the ancient, lived-in atmosphere of The Great Citadel.

Banners should support orientation without turning the room into a diagram.

## Material Rule

Great Hall banners should be made from aged fabric, woven cloth, leather-backed textile, or heavy ceremonial material.

They may include:

- Embroidery
- Faded dye
- Worn edges
- Patina
- Repairs
- Brass or bronze hanging rods
- Wooden brackets
- Subtle stitched symbols

They should never look newly printed, glossy, synthetic, or modern.

## Placement Rule

Banners may hang:

- Above or beside major doors
- Between stone columns
- Along upper galleries
- Near realm-related alcoves or shelves
- From wooden beams or brass rods

Banners should be physically attached to the architecture.

They should not float in space.

## Symbol Rule

Banners should use symbols more than text.

Preferred banner symbols include:

- Science Door: astrolabe, atom-like diagram, celestial instrument, compass geometry
- History Door: hourglass, archive mark, scroll, old timeline symbol
- Philosophy Door: column, open hand, balance, question mark-like motif, temple form
- Arts Door: palette, lyre, mask, brush, carved flourish
- Media Door: play triangle, illuminated frame, storytelling panel, signal motif
- Personal Door: human silhouette, mirror, hearth, memory emblem
- Inventor's Door: gear, tool, mechanical diagram, brass apparatus
- Foundational Door: Citadel outline, threshold mark, root symbol, return emblem

Text may appear, but it should be carved, stitched, or inscribed as part of the banner design.

## Color Rule

Banner colors should be muted, aged, and natural.

Preferred qualities:

- Deep faded blues
- Forest greens
- Oxidized bronze tones
- Wine red
- Ochre
- Charcoal
- Weathered gold
- Brown leather tones

Avoid bright modern colors, neon colors, or overly clean heraldic designs.

## Scale Rule

Banners should add warmth and human scale to the Great Hall.

They should soften the stone architecture and make the room feel inhabited, studied, and cared for.

They should not dominate the room or obscure the major architectural features.

## Aging Rule

Every banner should show evidence of time.

Acceptable signs of age include:

- Faded fabric
- Frayed lower edges
- Hand repairs
- Stitched patches
- Uneven dye
- Smoke darkening
- Dust
- Slight sagging from age

Banners should feel preserved, not pristine.

## Orientation Rule

When a Great Hall rendering faces a specific direction, visible banners should correspond only to the visible forward hemisphere.

For example, when facing north, visible banners may relate to:

- Science
- History
- Philosophy

Banners related to doors behind the viewer should not be shown.

## Canonical Principle

Banners in the Great Hall are memory-bearing architectural textiles.

They should help the visitor feel oriented inside The Great Citadel while preserving mystery, age, warmth, and discovery.
